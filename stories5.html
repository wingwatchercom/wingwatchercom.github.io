"Black Lighting For Moths"
By Dave and Judy Pelletier.



Moths are some of the most beautiful creatures on the planet. Sure, some people get the skin crawlies or heebie-jeebies from them, but they are quite harmless. When we first started this website, birds were the most important creatures to photograph. And then, after months of concentrating on birds, we decided to capture other winged critters. Hence, "Wing Watchers" expanded itself without changing its name.

Moths, or Lepidoptera as the pros call them, come in many shapes, sizes, and colors. There are over 10,000 species in North America but how many will the average person see? Some moths will travel about during the day but most are wanderers of the night. After a lot of research to find ID's of the few moths we had photographed, we discovered a great way to attract these beauties to us. Thanks to a tip that we got from John Himmelman's "Connecticut Moths" website, we learned how to "black light" for Lepidoptera. There is a link to Mr. Himmelmann's site on our Crawly Things page. You ought to go see his photos, simply awesome.


So here's our minor variation to John's idea. We went to the local hardware store and bought a screw-in type black light bulb and screwed it right in place of the normal outside light. We hear that moths are really attracted to this type of light and will come in from the darkness to visit the black light for awhile or for the night. We then hung a nice size piece of white cloth (a piece of an old sheet) down from the face boards under the gutters. At night, the black light seems to radiate through the sheet with a nice bluish-purple glow.


We were amazed at what happened. Without the lighting system we found two or three moths on our outside wall, occasionally. Now we have what seems like hundreds flying around the light and many of them will land on the sheet or wall to spend the night. We don't have the equipment to photograph at night so it works out well if we get out there with the camera real early, before the birds make a tasty breakfast of them. And they do, I'm sorry to say.
