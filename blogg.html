3/24/09 - Well, it has been a little while since I made an entry in the Blogg section. Sorry, it is hard to keep pumping out information or stories at a regular rate. Especially when you really aren't a writer to start with.

Let's make this page about a pesky creature that is as beautiful as it is helpful. That's right, the Blue Jay. You all know what I mean, don't you? They can drive you nuts when they come in large or even small groups with all the noise and mischief they bring with them.

It's also known that Blue Jays eat the eggs of other birds and any hatchlings that are in a nest. But the good that they do for mankind, nature, and the whole world masks the bad that they do. Let me tell you some facts from my resources, which I will list later for you. Jays are in the family of birds called Corvids and are cousins to the American Crow.

And according to one of the great books I use, The Audubon Society Encyclopedia of North American Birds, this bird's diet is the boon to mankind. The Blue Jay is omnivorous, which means that they will eat about anything. Among these many types of foods are the insects and caterpillars that it just loves to eat. Do you remember the serious Gypsy Moth caterpillar invasion here in New England?
The caterpillars were defoliating many kinds of trees at a quick rate. I believe that nasty event would have hurt the Earth a lot worse if that particular larvae wasn't one of Mr. Blue Jay's favorites. You see, leaves suck in or filter carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, process it, the release oxygen back into the air we breathe.

I don't know what you believe, but I believe that our Blue Jay is one of the most important birds we have.

Here are some dimensions and such for the Blue Jay:
I am no scientist so I will use my reference book, again, in order that the facts will be accurate according to the book and not my guess. This guy is located all over North America but mainly east of the Rocky Mountains. It is in the range of 11 to 12.5 inches in length and has a wingspan of 15.75 to 17.5 inches long. The coloring of this species is awesome, as you can see by the photo below. It is a very cunning as well as inquisitive and intellegent type of bird, most Corvids are.

When they travel in small flocks, if one spots an Owl on its daytime roost, it will signal the rest of the flock and they will harass it for awhile. All in a days work I guess.

Here is just some of the things that Blue Jays eat:
To start with, and here is another plus for the Jay, it eats nuts from oak and beech trees plus many other trees, bushes and plants. Often it will carry two nuts or seeds for burying and as they forget where they are, a tree or new plant will sprout. Neat, huh? Here's more - they also eat many kinds of berries, insects like many types of caterpillars and insect eggs. You could find them eating small fish, frogs, bats, small birds and eggs, spiders, and it will come to feeders for whatever you have like suet, seeds, or fruits. Quite a bird for having such a bad reputation!
